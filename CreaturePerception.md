Simulation perspective
1. A creature perception/detection model (do this first). Give creatures sight (reuse the FOV you already have, run symmetrically from the creature), plus a noise channel (moving, fighting, and harvesting emit noise; sprinting emits more) and optionally scent that lingers on tiles. Raptors then transition between unaware → suspicious → tracking → engaged based on what they actually perceive, instead of reading the player's coordinates. This is the keystone: it's a foundational sim primitive, it makes your best existing system (the raptor FSM) finally pay off, it gives every piece of worldgen cover real meaning, and it's the prerequisite for the biggest gameplay pillar below. Relatively contained to build because the FOV math already exists.
2. Creature ecology / drives. Right now one archetype (Raptor) carries the entire threat model, and WanderingCreature just does a random walk with no needs. A lightweight drive system — hunger, patrol, flee, herd — plus one herbivore type and a second predator would make the island feel inhabited rather than like a raptor-delivery mechanism. Herbivores also give raptors something to hunt other than you, which (combined with #1) creates emergent moments where the pack is distracted.
3. A real damage model. CombatResolver deals a flat DefaultAttackDamage = 8 to everything, every time — no variance, no source stats, no hit location or injury. Even a modest model (attacker/defender stats feeding damage, a crit/miss band, and a lingering injury state like a bleeding or limping status) turns combat from a coin that always lands the same into something with texture.
Gameplay perspective
1. Close the progression loop — it's currently a lie. CharacterProgress stores Level, Experience, and Attributes; they're saved, loaded, and rendered on a character sheet — but XP is never awarded, Level never increases, and Attributes are never read by any system. Killing a raptor advances a first_kill quest and grants nothing. Wire the circuit end to end: objectives/kills/survival grant XP → XP grants levels → levels/attributes feed into the damage model and max health/energy. This is low-effort relative to payoff because every piece already exists in isolation; you're just connecting them.
2. Make items and inventory matter. You have three items (Wood, Berry, Rope). Rope is actually used (rope descent), but berries do literally nothing — they sit in a list. Give the player verbs: eat a berry to heal, craft wood+rope into a snare or a fence-repair, set a distraction to pull raptors off your trail. This connects inventory → survival → the raptor sim, and it pairs beautifully with #1 on the simulation side (a distraction is only meaningful if raptors perceive rather than know).
3. Stealth as a pillar (falls out of simulation #1 for free). Once creatures perceive instead of know, the player gets real decisions: break line of sight in the foliage, move slow to stay quiet, use tunnels to reposition unseen, choose whether to sprint-and-be-heard toward the evac point as the pressure clock closes. Your pressure system (evac window, storms, power failures, blocked routes) already creates the macro tension; perception creates the micro tension to match it.
