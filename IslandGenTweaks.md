1. Domain warp is far too timid — this is your highest-leverage fix. In IslandShapeDefaults.CreateNublar(), DomainWarp is Frequency = 1.6, Amplitude = 0.07. In the normalized blob space your ellipses live in, 0.07 is a barely-perceptible wobble, so the underlying ellipse arcs survive and the eye reads "welded ovals." Domain warp is the best tool you have for organic SDF shapes because it distorts the whole field coherently — it's exactly the missing meso band. Push amplitude to roughly 0.18–0.28 and add a second, lower-frequency warp octave (a big slow warp around frequency 0.8 for overall lobing, plus your existing ~1.6 for headlands). NoiseUtility.DomainWarp already takes large/medium/small strengths, so you can lean on it harder rather than adding code. This one change alone will move you a long way.
2. The coastal cellular automata is adding the "chewed" high-frequency texture. CoastlineVariationStage.ApplyCoastalCellularAutomata runs 3 iterations of per-cell add/remove based on landNeighbors thresholds and per-cell FBM. CA at single-cell granularity roughens the edge at the pixel scale — it fights the smooth SDF you carefully composed and produces the staticky trim you can see in the middle profile above. CA is a great tool for binary cave maps, but on a coastline it usually injects the wrong frequency. I'd either drop CellularAutomataIterations to 0–1 and let domain warp do the work, or replace the add/remove rule with a majority smoothing rule (round the coast toward its neighbors) instead of the current noise-gated toggling, which currently roughens as much as it smooths.
3. Your coastline detail is uniform, so it reads as uniform. In IslandMaskStage, the shore-band detail is Fbm × CoastlineDetail.Amplitude with a constant amplitude (0.025) everywhere. Real coasts vary: rocky headlands are jagged, sandy bays are smooth. Modulate the detail amplitude by a low-frequency "ruggedness" field sampled per-coast-position — multiply CoastlineDetail.Amplitude by something like 0.3 + 1.4 * Fbm(ruggednessSeed, wx*0.5, wy*0.5). Now some coasts are glassy and some are broken, which paradoxically looks more natural than a uniform medium everywhere. Bias it to subtract slightly more than it adds, too — erosion removes land; it rarely grows symmetric peninsulas.
4. Procedural inlets are round bites, not tapered coves. CarveCoastalInlets → CarveEllipse cuts rotated ellipses at high-noise-score coast cells. An ellipse scoop looks like a melon-baller took it out. Two improvements: taper the carve so it's wide at the mouth and narrows inland (scale radiusAlongShore down along the inward normal), and — bigger win — carve inlets where your rivers actually meet the coast rather than at noise-score peaks. You already generate rivers (riverCount: 10 in island.yaml); river-mouth inlets correlate the coast with the terrain, which is a strong naturalness cue. Right now the inlets are placed independently of everything else, which the eye picks up on.
5. The blob skeleton is still visible through everything. Three ellipses smooth-unioned at Smoothness ≈ 0.18–0.20 gives that metaball silhouette. Once you turn up the warp (#1) this largely hides itself, but you can help it by adding a per-blob angular radius warp inside EllipseSdf.Evaluate — perturb rx/ry as a function of Atan2(rotY, rotX) with a little FBM so no single blob is a clean ellipse. Even a ±15% angular wobble breaks the tell.
If I were doing this in order: turn up domain warp (#1), knock CA down to 0–1 iteration (#2), then add the ruggedness modulation (#3) and re-test. Those three are cheap, low-risk, and target the exact middle-band gap the diagram shows. The inlet/river work (#4) and per-blob warp (#5) are the polish pass after the silhouette already reads right.
